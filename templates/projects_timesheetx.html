Refreshing TimesheetX student experience

TimesheetX (conceptual project) · Product design · 4 weeks

Context & Problem

TimesheetX is a comprehensive web solution for student employment timesheet needs. It is used and trusted by hundreds of higher education institutions and millions of students across the US today.

Students use TimesheetX every other week to report time worked with supervisors to get paid. However, its student experience falls short of satisfactory. Students constantly report frustration and difficulty of use.

Solution & Results

I conducted research and redesigned TimesheetX’s main feature to improve the student experience.

  • 100% of testing users completed given tasks, saying it’s very easy to use.
  • Task completion time was reduced by 60%.
  • 100% of testing users said they want to use the redesign instead of the current one.

Design process ↓

Usability audit

I set out to try TimesheetX for myself to understand first-hand the struggles that my fellow students are facing. Besides the uninspiring and outdated UI, the website is a non-responsive, out-of-the-box solution without any information architecture or customization to streamline the timesheet workflow.

Insights from students

I sat down with 5 student employees from my college to understand their working/timesheet habits and their problems with TimesheetX. Here are the most common insights:

First, I see a shift in user behaviors with online shopping:

  • “Although I want to use TimesheetX on the phone, I have to use it on laptops or desktops because it’s easier to use.”
  • “I struggled to locate the timesheets the first few times...”
  • “... I often make mistakes when entering date and time.”
  • “I’m disappointed that it’s non-responsive and visually outdated.”

Another valuable insight is that reentering the same weekly work times is a pain point for students with a fixed work schedule. However, since this is a high impact-high effort task, I prioritized other tasks first to stay within the time budget.

Objectives

After synthesizing and prioritizing all insights, I clearly understood the problem.

Optimizing TimesheetX for mobile devices, improving timesheet wayfinding and entry interaction, and renewing the visual design will improve the TimesheetX student experience.

1. Improving timesheet wayfinding

Information architecture

TimesheetX currently lacks information architecture and visual support, using only 2 categories, “Information” and “Other Links.” The main feature, “Enter your Timesheet,” is buried among other links, making it difficult to find. Moreover, it takes the students through several pages to reach a timesheet. To improve wayfinding, I ran a cart sort with 5 participants to group the links into useful categories.

My Timesheets

I was determined to bring the timesheets outside to make them more prominently accessible. Since students mainly use TimesheetX to report worked hours and rarely review Resources or past jobs, I decided to show only current timesheets on the homepage.

2. Improving timesheet entry design

Timesheet layout

From my conversations with students, filling out timesheets must be the biggest pain point. The current design is a spreadsheet without any consideration for touch target. So, I also tested some new layouts with the students:

  • Option 1 drew inspiration from the calendar view. Students press and drag to add a timeframe.
  • Option 2 is similar to the current layout, where students add the dates themselves from a dropdown menu. It divides a 2-week pay period into 2 rows because of a specific pain point: Students often select the wrong date from a list of 14 days.
  • Option 3 displays all dates in a pay period, so students only have to enter the time worked.

Option 2 won with 5 votes because:

  • It’s easier to get an overall view of the whole timesheet, as opposed to option 1.
  • It gives them the flexibility only to add the dates they worked and multiple shifts in a day, whereas in option 3 they would leave some dates unfilled and cannot add more than 1 shift in a date.
  • Scrolling vertically on a mobile phone is more natural than scrolling horizontally in option 3.

Entry interaction

With the current design, students must select dates and times from long lists that are highly error-prone. I explored different design patterns for time entry on mobile devices:

After evaluating, I adopted both options 2 and 3.

Depending on jobs/supervisors, students would either round up their work time or report the exact time by the minute. Option 2 works well to select time by increments, while the keyboard in option 3 allows the flexibility to enter an exact number. Option 1, although provides a nice touch gesture, takes up more screen estate.

3. UI makeover

It’s clear from the get-go that TimesheetX needs a new look. Inspired by TimesheetX’s current color scheme and minimal design, I created a new logo that appeals to students and a clean, simplistic interface incorporating graphical elements to reduce users’ cognitive load.

4. Testing

I applied the new style to the wireframes from ideation and created a prototype. I tested it with 6 users using the following metrics:

  • Task completion rate & time
  • User rating of ease of entering a timesheet
  • User rating of the new UI design

The redesign received very positive feedback without any significant problems.

Learnings ↓

Designing for mobile first

This project led me to prioritize navigation and interaction, which I consider key design elements for mobile devices. As there are space restrictions on smaller screens, I ensured that the most critical content, in this case - timesheets, are prominently displayed since those are the ones students actively look for. It was delightful to study the design patterns and iterations on mobile devices that we don’t always notice.

Designing less for more

Reducing users' cognitive load is my biggest holistic goal for this project. While students can still complete their tasks with the current design, it constantly puts even senior students under a cognitive strain. By reorganizing content and taking out unnecessary information, the redesign greatly improves the experience.

On a technical note, it also reinforces the importance of following visual design principles, especially visual hierarchy, to aid user navigation.
