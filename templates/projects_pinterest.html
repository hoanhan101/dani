Turning Pins into Purchases with an In-app Shopping Feature

Pinterest is an image-sharing and social media service. For a long time, Pinterest has been the starting point for shopping inspiration.

The Problem

Pinterest isn’t living up to its full social commerce potential. There is no seamless shopping experience that allow customers to buy directly in the app.

My Role

UX/UI Designer, individual project

Project duration

4 weeks/80 hours

Diving into an intensive research sprint

Kickoff

First, I had to ask myself: “Does a shopping feature align with Pinterest’s values?” and “What values does it offer the business and its customers?”

With that as a starting point, I dived into desk research to find answers and frame the scope of the project.

Pinterest’s statistics reveal a huge opportunity for social commerce.

Besides the fact that most users use Pinterest for planning and purchase inspiration, over 60% of them are more open to new brands and new products; therefore, they are a great audience for advertising. Ads also made up two-thirds of Pinterest revenue in 2021. For retailers, they reported a 3x conversion rate and 2x return on ad spend on Pinterest. Source

Interestingly, Pinterest has launched and terminated an in-app shopping feature in 2018. I couldn’t get an answer from Pinterest, but I suspect potential causes to be scalability, security issues, consideration for brand value, insufficient execution, or they simply tested the feature too soon. Source

The feature (done right!) would maintain Pinterest’s brand value while decrease the time it takes from discovery to purchase and create a win-win-win situation for customers, retailers, and Pinterest.

With the time budget and user research resources I have for this project, I decided to design for customers first.

Insights from competitors

Facebook and Instagram’s shopping features constantly popped up during my desk research as they are the leading platforms in social commerce. I needed to know what they and other Pinterest’s competitors are doing to identify their strengths and weaknesses and spot any gaps in the industry.

Social commerce is all about user-generated content driving community-driven commerce.

Social media platforms that have a strong community-driven culture performs especially well with social commerce. Although Pinterest users (Pinners) don’t interact with each other the way they would on other social media platforms, it turns out to be one of Pinterest’s key competitive advantage. The platform’s unique culture creates a sense of positivity and authenticity that is essential to building a loyal customer base and confident shoppers.

Insights from customers

Now that I have armed myself with adequate knowledge about social commerce, it was time to get out there and talk with customers. I interviewed 8 social media users who regularly shop online to find answers to key questions:

  • What are their social media and shopping habits?
  • What are their pain points when online shopping with and without social media?
  • What are their needs, wants, and expectation for an in-app shopping feature?

Social commerce could interrupt the social media experience.

One insight stood out from the interviews was that there is a gap in devices used for social media and online shopping. While all participants use social media (Pinterest included) on a mobile device, six of them shop online on a laptop/desktop for ease of use. Moreover, products posted on social media often have insufficient information that requires further researching on the web before making purchase decisions.

Shoppers tend to shop at only one brand at a time with a few go-to brands.

Although inspiring products come everywhere, inconvenience when navigating different e-commerce websites and buyer’s guilt when making more than one purchases have been stopping customers from exploring new brands and products. This is one of the reasons why Amazon has been so successful - by allowing customers to buy everything from anywhere all at once.

Prioritizing insights to conceptualize

The journey

With plenty of insights, I needed to prioritize and synthesize them to form a strategy. I recognized a few patterns that were later turned into two user personas of “light user” (uses Pinterest occassionally for finding inspiration when needed) and “heavy user” (uses Pinterest requently both for inspiration and entertainment) and mapped into user journey. I placed more focus on the heavy user since they are more align with Pinterest’s value.

Finding size/fit and checkout are the main reasons shoppers abandon their shopping bag.

Among several overlaps in the two personas, difficulties in finding size/fit and complicated checkout process stood out. Customers often struggle to find the right size for their body from inadequate information and assistant, which causes hesitation to buy. On another note, a complicated checkout process is not only time-consuming and tiring, but also creates an association with lack of security and unprofessionalism.

From problems to solutions

These findings begged the question, how might we make sure customers feel safe and confident throughout the shopping process?

The three-phase journey (in the journey map) was then translated into three focus areas, each of which is wireframed into several concepts and tested with eight users. Since the mobile phone is where the social media experience starts, it is the device I designed for.

Working with a design system

UI design

Although Pinterest’s Gestalt design system is published online, it’s no copy-paste for people outside the team. So how can I design efficiently without the original design file to steal from? The old style tile come to the rescue! After gathering all elements I need from the wireframes and created them according to Pinterest’s design guide, I’m ready to go.
