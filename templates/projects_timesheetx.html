Redesigning an Outdated Timesheet Website for Student Employees

TimesheetX is a comprehensive web solution for student employment timesheet needs. It is used and trusted by hundreds of higher education institutions and millions of students across the US today.

The Problem

Students use TimesheetX every other week to report time worked with supervisors to get paid. However, its student experience falls short of satisfactory. Students constantly report frustration and difficulty of use.

My Role

Product design • Solo project

Disclaimer: I am not affiliated with TimesheetX. This case study is an exploratory learning experience on a product that I deeply care about.

Project duration

4 weeks • April 2022

Diverging to discover

Kickoff — Usability audit

After getting an overview of TimesheetX, I set out to try it for myself to understand first-hand the struggles that my fellow students are facing. First, as an average human being and second, as a designer with a checklist of usability heuristics.

Usability audit reveals major issues in user control, consistency, and efficiency of use.

Besides the uninspiring and outdated UI, the website is an out-of-the-box solution without any information architecture or customization to streamline the timesheet workflow.

Insights from student employees

I sat down with 5 student employees to find answer to key questions:

  • What are students’ working and timesheet habits?
  • What are their pain points when reporting timesheets?
  • What do student employees need and want?

Students couldn’t use the website on their preferred device.

Although the students’ flow usually starts on the phone - reading email reminders of approaching timesheet submission deadlines, they have to switch to a laptop because TimesheetX’s mobile experience is too limited.

It doesn’t get easier over time.

Even senior students struggle with navigation to locate their jobs and wanted timesheets. The website presents redundant features and information that often limit students’ control and require high cognitive load to skim through.

All students want a better way to extract their schedule to a timesheet.

Currently, all students rely on Google Calendar and memory to fill out worked shifts. They express an interest in a feature that would allow them to save a schedule to have it prefilled and editable if necessary. After afinity mapping all the user insights, I decided that this feature was potentially a project on its own and out of my time budget. It’s high-impact but also high-effort.

Insights from competitors

After talking to students, I couldn’t help but wonder what other timesheet services colleges are using for student employments. After identifying TimesheetX’s competitors and reviewing them, I found a big gap in the field.

Many colleges still use manual PDF and physical timesheets. Others use outdated services similar to TimesheetX. And the more modern services often offer too many features at much higher prices.

TimesheetX has an unique opportunity to renew and optimize for institution’s employment needs.

Framing problem areas

The current user experience

With all the insights gathered, I prioritized and mapped them out to identify problem areas and opportunities for improvements.

Multiple touchpoints are required to successfully submit a single timesheet.

Besides switching devices, students often need additional communication with peers and supervisors to override unnessary work allotment restrictions, which leads to frustration and wasted time.

Date and time entry is the most painful part.

Student have to scroll through and select from a list of date and time (by the minute) for every shift. This experience is especially agonizing on the phone since the website is non-responsive with no customization for a touch screen.

From problems to solutions

So how might we?

  • Reduce navigation time?
  • Optimize TimesheetX for mobile devices?
  • Improve date and time entry interactions?

I brainstormed different potential solutions for other problem areas identified and translated these solutions into several wireframe concepts for preference testing.

Only show relevant information.

Since students rarely need to review past jobs, old timesheets, and help documentation, this information is stored away in easy-to-find categories. A card sort exercise with students also reveals that they prefer all help documentation in one “Resources” category.

Flexible timesheet layout.

Instead of a calendar view with a designated time slot for each day, students prefer to add shifts themselves. It gives them the flexibility of only adding dates they worked and multiple shifts a day when applicable. Plus, it saves screen space and works well with vertical scrolling, which all students prefer.

The UI makeover

Logo and style

Now that the new website has had a strong skeleton, it’s time for the exciting part! It’s clear from the get-go that TimesheetX needs a new look. I needed to design for students while keeping it professional for institutions.

Inspired by TimesheetX’s current color scheme and minimal design, I created a new logo that appeal to the young adults and a clean, simplistic interface incorporating graphical elements to reduce users’ cognitive load.

The new logo and style were then applied to the wireframes to become high-fidelity prototype ready for testing.

The results

Prominent access to jobs

The new information architecture provides instant access to relevant jobs and timesheets as soon as students logged into their accounts.

All secondary information can be found in easy-to-access categories from the navigation bar.

Customized layouts and interactions

The two-week timesheet period is broken down into two rows for easier recalling and avoid selecting the wrong dates.

Students are able add and remove shifts quickly. Date and time selection resembles the alarm setting design on mobile devices for ease of use.

Giving control back to the students

Students can edit their timesheets after submission, as long as supervisors haven’t finalized them yet.

Unnecessary limitations about work allotment are removed to give students more control and accountability of their timesheets.

Last thoughts

If I had more time, I would

  • Design a work schedule and timesheet auto-fill feature
  • Design more screens for a more realistic prototype
  • Conduct more user testings and keep refining the design based on test results

Moving forward

This project has led me to connect with my fellow students and talked about the struggles that were often brushed off. But it also makes me feel blue: Although students make up the majority of TimesheetX users, they didn’t get a say in choosing the product in the first place and feel forced to use an outdated product.

So moving forward, I plan to bring my design to TimesheetX and hopefully see them renew the product in the future!
