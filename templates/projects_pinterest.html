Shopping without leaving Pinterest

Pinterest (conceptual project) · Product design · 4 weeks

Context & Problem

Pinterest is an image-sharing and social media service. For a long time, it has been the starting point for shopping inspiration for hundreds of millions of users every month.

Pinterest has an opportunity to offer users a seamless shopping experience. The current user flow requires customers to leave Pinterest to visit sellers’ websites, potentially exposing them to unfamiliar websites' usability problems.

Solution & Results

I conducted research and designed an in-app shopping feature that allows customers to buy products from multiple sellers at once.

  • 100% of testing users completed given tasks, saying it felt convenient and engaging.
  • 62% of testing users said they would use this feature in real life.
  • 50% of testing Pinners said they would recommend this feature to their Pinner friends.

Design process ↓

Market research

I learned that Pinterest’s business model heavily relies on user interactions with retailer ads to eventually convert to buyers.

Compare to other big social media platforms like Instagram and Facebook (who also put a focus on social commerce), Pinterest’s unique culture creates a sense of positivity and authenticity that is essential to creating confident shoppers. Having a seamless shopping experience would save users’ time and effort, help retailers get sales quicker, and increase Pinterest’s revenue.

Pinterest can be arguably considered a unique platform and not social media. But since Pinterest shares many users with the 3 platforms above, I still looked into their social commerce features. Social commerce has seen a considerable rise, especially with the pandemic, and platforms focus strongly on online shopping.

User Insights

To find out what would improve the shopping experience on Pinterest, I interviewed 10 social media users (7 use Pinterest) who regularly shop online.

First, I see a shift in user behaviors with online shopping:

  • All 10 users have discovered and saved new brands and products on social media. Among them, 4 have bought directly from Instagram or Facebook on the phone.
  • 6 users recently online shopped on the phone for the first time.

Here are the most common insights:

  • “I have to search for specific things to see the Product Pins [on Pinterest].”
  • “... I haven’t bought anything [from social media] because it’s inconvenient to navigate new websites on the phone.”
  • “... I try to buy everything I need from one seller because I feel less guilty making one purchase and only have to check out once. ”
  • “Complicated checkout makes me abandon the shopping bag.”

Goal & Objectives

From a pretty open-ended goal, I got a clearer picture of the project after synthesizing research data.

From here, it became clear that an in-app shopping feature would solve users' problems navigating unfamiliar e-commerce sites by creating a unified experience across all sellers. Offering users quick access to products while allowing them to shop all products at once with a simple checkout process would improve Pinterest's shopping experience.

1. Improving product navigation

Shop tab

I decided to replace Messages and Updates with a dedicated Shop tab with a product search bar for several reasons:

  • It provides clear and prominent access to products.
  • Pinterest has a large selection of products due to its primary usage for finding new products and shopping inspiration.
  • I learned from user interviews that users rarely chat or view updates on Pinterest.

Categories

Since Categories is one of the most used features for product navigation, I ideated different layouts to display this information.

  • Option 1 displays the Category menu and Shop-by categories in a carousel as a part of the sticky top nav.
  • Option 2 displays the options in cards as a part of the content.
  • Option 3 stores categories in a hamburger menu similar to a standard e-commerce site on mobile view. The Shop-by categories are removed and will be included in sorts & filters once users search for something.

After testing with 8 users, option 1 won with 5 votes because:

  • It displays all options clearly without taking much screen space.
  • People like to have these options available as they browse.
  • While a category hamburger menu is a standard for an e-commerce site, it might not be easily recognizable in the app context.

2. Allowing to shop from multiple sellers at once

Shopping bag

I had to consider a logical and scalable design to display products when a user buys multiple products from multiple sellers. Therefore, I also sketched out different layouts to test with users.

  • Option 1 organizes products by the sellers. Product information is visible and editable as users often make changes in the shopping bag.
  • Option 2 also groups products by sellers but only displays product thumbnails for memorability purposes. Users can tab on each group to enter another screen where they can view and edit product details.
  • Option 3 puts a visual emphasis on Products and lists out all products with the seller's name below each.

After testing, option 1 won with 7 votes because:

  • Since all products from one seller will be shipped together, it makes more sense that the visual hierarchy also translates that.
  • Grouping products by sellers give them a sense of organization.
  • They prefer to be able to edit products quickly without extra tapping.

Since the checkout displays have similar results, I won't discuss them in detail here.

3. Defining a simple checkout flow

3-step checkout

I learned from users that although they expect to go through all steps of a standard checkout flow, they conceptualize it as having 3 steps. From here, I designed a checkout process according to this finding.

4. Testing

After turning all wireframes into high-fidelity prototypes using Pinterest Gestalt design system, I tested it with 8 users using the following metrics:

  • Task completion rate
  • User rating of ease of navigation
  • User rating of comfort of placing an order

Overall, the feature received very positive feedback and there wasn't any major problems.

Learnings ↓

Design system

During this project, I learned the benefit of using design patterns to communicate visual hierarchy and grew to appreciate design systems. It makes keeping the feature's interface consistent with the app less challenging and allows me to design the interface more quickly and spend more time on small tests. The latter has proven very effective as the MVP didn't have any major problems right after the first usability test.

Start small

When I started this project, it felt overwhelming. Pinterest has impressive statistics with hundreds of millions of users a month. It led me to consider not only users' goals but also those of Pinterest and retailers. By taking the time to synthesize research data and prioritizing the right focus areas, I could design a successful feature MVP with fewer iterations needed.
