Turning Pins into Purchases with an In-app Shopping Feature

Pinterest is an image-sharing and social media service. For a long time, Pinterest has been the starting point for shopping inspiration.

The Problem

Pinterest isn’t living up to its full social commerce potential. There is no seamless shopping experience that allows customers to buy directly in the app.

My Role

Product design • Solo project

Disclaimer: I am not affiliated with TimesheetX. This case study is an exploratory learning experience on a product that I care about deeply.

Project duration

4 weeks • May 2022

Diving into an intensive research sprint

Kickoff — Market research

First, I had to ask myself: “Does a shopping feature align with Pinterest’s values?” and “What values does it offer the business and its customers?”

With that as a starting point, I dived into desk research to find answers and frame the scope of the project.

Pinterest’s statistics reveal a huge opportunity for social commerce.

Besides the fact that most users use Pinterest for planning and purchase inspiration, over 60% of them are more open to new brands and new products; therefore, they are a great audience for advertising. Ads also made up two-thirds of Pinterest revenue in 2021. Retailers reported a 3x conversion rate and a 2x return on ad spend on Pinterest.

Interestingly, Pinterest launched and terminated an in-app shopping feature in 2018. I couldn’t get an answer from Pinterest, but I suspect potential causes to be scalability, security issues, consideration for brand value, insufficient execution, or they tested the feature too soon. Source

The feature (done right!) would maintain Pinterest’s brand value while decreasing the time it takes from discovery to purchase and creating a win-win situation for customers, retailers, and Pinterest.

With the time budget and user research resources I have for this project, I decided to design for customers first.

Insights from competitors

Facebook and Instagram’s shopping features constantly popped up during my desk research as they are the leading platforms in social commerce. I needed to know what they and other Pinterest competitors were doing to identify their strengths and weaknesses and spot any gaps in the industry.

Social commerce is all about user-generated content driving community-driven commerce.

Social media platforms with a strong community-driven culture perform especially well with social commerce. Although Pinterest users (Pinners) don’t interact with each other the way they would on other social media platforms, it turns out to be one of Pinterest’s key competitive advantages. The platform’s unique culture creates a sense of positivity and authenticity essential to building a loyal customer base and confident shoppers.

Insights from customers

Now that I have armed myself with adequate knowledge about social commerce, it was time to get out there and talk with customers. I interviewed 8 social media users who regularly shop online to find answers to key questions:

  • What are their social media and shopping habits?
  • What are their pain points when shopping online with and without social media?
  • What are their needs, wants, and expectations for an in-app shopping feature?

Social commerce could interrupt the social media experience.

One insight that stood out from the interviews was that there is a gap in devices used for social media and online shopping. While all participants use social media (Pinterest included) on a mobile device, 6 shop online on a laptop/desktop for ease of use. Moreover, products posted on social media often have insufficient information that requires further research on the web before making purchase decisions.

Furthermore, users fear that shopping means more ads. While they don’t mind ads so much as if correctly targeted, irrelevant marketing content makes the app look like it is trying too hard to sell and moving away from the brand’s values.

Shoppers shop at only one brand at a time, with a few go-to brands.

Although inspiring products come everywhere, inconvenience when navigating different e-commerce websites and buyer’s guilt when making more than one purchase have been stopping customers from exploring new brands and products.

Prioritizing insights to conceptualize

The user journey

With plenty of insights, I needed to prioritize and synthesize them to form a strategy. I recognized a few patterns that were later turned into two user personas of “light user” (uses Pinterest occasionally for finding inspiration when needed) and “heavy user” (uses Pinterest frequently both for inspiration and entertainment) and mapped into the user journey. I focused more on the heavy user since they are statistically shown to shop more often.

Finding size/fit and checkout are the main reasons shoppers abandon shopping bags.

Among several overlaps in the two personas, difficulties in finding size/fit and a complicated checkout process stood out. Customers often struggle to find the right size for their body from inadequate information and assistance, which causes hesitation to buy.

On another note, a complicated checkout process is time-consuming and tiring and creates an association with a lack of security and unprofessionalism.

From problems to solutions

These findings beg the question, how might we ensure customers feel safe and confident throughout the shopping process?

Design opportunity #1: How might we make the shopping undisruptive to the social media shopping experience?

Needed to embed my shopping feature seamlessly within the current IA, I ran a card sort and organized a high-level app map to show where the new features would live. Since the mobile device is where the social media experience happens, it is the device I designed for.

All shoppable pins will live in a designated “Shop” section that is unintrusive to the current browse-and-save user flow. It would also create more opportunities for ads and marketing content since people who visit this section are ready to explore products.

Design opportunity #2: How might we ensure users have adequate information to feel confident placing an order?

I have gathered all product and seller information users need for decision-making from user interviews. I would also suggest retailers follow a checklist of the required information to ensure consistency throughout the app:

  • Size guide, size chart, and model measurements
  • Shipping methods and estimated delivery date
  • Return and exchanges policy

On Pinterest’s end, we need to be transparent about data security and let them know how their personal information and payment information will be treated:

  • Will the information be shared with sellers?
  • How do we aim to protect customers’ data?
  • Do we offer buyer protection?

Working with a design system

UI design

Although Pinterest’s Gestalt design system is published online, it’s no copy-paste for people outside the team. So how can I design efficiently without the original design file to steal from? The old style tile comes to the rescue! After gathering all elements I need from the wireframes and creating them according to Pinterest’s design guide, I’m ready to go.

During this project, I grew to appreciate design systems. Not only does it make keeping the feature's interface consistent with the app less challenging, but it also allows me to allocate more time and head space to the feature's functionality.

After my vision came to life, it was ready for testing and iterating for the “final" design.

The results

An unintrusive social commerce experience

The shopping feature lies seamlessly within the app that won’t interfere with users’ social media experience.

Customized categories and filters for Pinners

Shopping categories are customized to Pinterest’s most searched items to give users prominent access to wanted products.

Shopping confidently without leaving the app

Each product comes with adequate information and sizing assistance to help users make purchase decisions effortlessly.

Checkout quickly and securely

Users can order multiple products from different sellers while checkout only once with a simple 3-step checkout.

Shipping and payment information is presented transparently to ease any security concerns.

Last thoughts

If I had more time, I would

  • Talk to more Pinners to get more unique insights into Pinterest's culture and values to create a more customized experience.
  • Conduct more user testings and keep refining the design based on test results

Brief reflection

This project has led me to consider not only users’ goals but also those of Pinterest and retailers. From researching Pinterest’s business model, I was able to make more efficient design decisions that would satisfy the common goals of the users, the company, and the clients.

During the design process, I wished I had a chance to talk to people from Pinterest to verify my hypotheses and gain insights that could change my design decisions. This has further inspired me to be a part of a team where we can grow while crafting rich and impactful experiences.
